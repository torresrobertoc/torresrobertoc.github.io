Accessibility... Who Cares?

(Originally posted August 2016)

Accessibility is often overlooked. In general terms, making your website/ application more accessible means you're making it easier for people with disabilities to use your product. The problem is that most projects lack clear accessibility objectives. This means that either stakeholders don't think it's important or they're lacking the knowledge to define accessibility policies. My goal for this post is to provide education about accessibility and to provide arguments for including accessibility into all project plans.

To start with, accessibility is important for every project. If you say, “Well, we don't have to worry about accessibility for our project because most of our users don't have disabilities,” that is analogous to saying we shouldn't have handicap parking spaces, wheelchair ramps, or bathroom handicap stalls because most people don't have disabilities. Obviously, we have decided as a society to make many accommodations for people with disabilities in the real world. If we can agree that the idea of denying people with disabilities accommodations in the real world is deplorable, why would we deny our users the same courtesy in our software?

Some might argue that there is a big difference between the difficulties that people with disabilities face in the real-world versus what users with disabilities face on the web. I would counter that point by arguing that the web has become so essential in this Information Age that the differences between access in the real world and access in the virtual world are becoming negligible. As a matter of fact, according to The New York Times, a federal court has recently decided that broadband internet should be considered a utility on par with telephone and electricity. Like it or not, all signs point to users expecting higher accessibility standards from their software products as well as increased accessibility regulations being enforced by governing bodies.

That being said, it's important to realize that any effort put into implementing accessibility is not wasted on the majority of users. On the contrary, implementing good accessibility practices into your project improves usability for all. For instance, many users might be affected by stress, fatigue, or environmental distractions as part of their normal, daily work activities which may diminish their cognitive and physical abilities. Have you ever visited a website after a long day and found yourself needing to squint or move closer just to read the text? If more websites implemented some basic accessibility practices, everyone's overall productivity would increase.

Additionally, time, expense, and effort are not valid excuses for neglecting accessibility policies. Just as software aspects like security, branding, and integrations have varying levels of implementation, accessibility can also be implemented in different degrees. Keep in mind that a minimum accessibility policy is far better than a complete lack of any accessibility policies.

So, who cares about Accessibility? We all should.
