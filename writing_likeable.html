Likeable Software

(Originally posted August 2016)

To this day, my favorite software-related quote rings true:

“If we want users to like our software we should design it to behave like a likeable person: respectful, generous and helpful.” - Alan Cooper (author of The Inmates are Running the Asylum)

Like so many truths, this seems obvious enough until you start to deconstruct its meaning. The first time I read this, I asked myself: Is it possible to make software like a person? If so, how do those “likeable” traits translate to software?

It's no secret that software has become an increasingly larger part of our everyday lives. We rely on our phones, vehicles and appliances to interact with us in more ways than ever before. As we continue making software that integrates with our personal lives, we tend to develop feelings (whether positive or negative) to our smart devices. For instance, have you ever had a customer service representative complain about their computer “acting up” or being “in one of its moods”? Do you know someone that “loves” their car because of some helpful features? It is human nature to anthropomorphize the things we interact with on a regular basis, therefore it is possible to make software like a person because of how we end up relating to it.

So, how do we make likeable software? “Likeability” is such a vague & complex concept for something as finite as software that we have to start with the idea of it being more of a guiding principle than a discrete step. Once we have that in our heads, we have to focus on the touchpoints our software has with users in order to seem “likeable”.

Here thus, is what I think likeable software would resemble at different touchpoints according to the traits of a likeable person:

  1. Respectful:

    1. The software respects a user's time by getting right to the point.
      • Use progressive disclosure to minimize the amount of information users see until they truly need it.
    2. The software respects a user's point of view by communicating via their lexicon.
      • Don't use terms that your users might not be familiar with.

  2. Generous:

    1. The software is generous with the actions a user can take to recover from mistakes.
      • Make it easy for users to undo actions.
    2. The software is generous with system data by telling users where it stands.
      • Make status information readily available.

  3. Helpful:

    1. The software helps users learn about the system so they can be more efficient.
      • Give tips liberally without interrupting a user's flow.
    2. The software helps by explaining why errors occurred using language most familiar to the user.
      • Avoid error messages that describe technical problems from the underlying code.
