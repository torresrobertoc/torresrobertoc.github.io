Form Validations

(Originally posted November 2016)

The most frustrating online experiences don't stem from clashing color schemes or overly verbose text, but instead from unfriendly or “unusable” form validations, notoriously from public sector websites and online banking. The validation of data is an essential part of software engineering yet prior to development, validation is rarely given more than a cursory glance, a mention, or worse yet - assumed to be implemented without further discussion.

Error messages and notifications occur during moments that we have a user's most-focused attention. From a user's perspective, they're in the middle of completing one of many tasks. They expect to see confirmation of an action (like submitting/saving) when instead they are prompted with errors they didn't know existed. Right-away users start feeling frustrated as they start trying to figure-out what they missed. As a user does this, they are even more focused on the screen than they were a minute ago because they're trying to reconcile their expectations with what actually occurred. It is at this moment that the user is most “connected” to the software. We should embrace these moments of “connection” and seize them as ways to make the best impression on the user. If we expect users to love our software, then when problems occur (like invalid or missing data) we should be willing to do some “hand-holding” to get them through what could be a frustrating experience.

Here are some tips for giving a best impression when validating form data:

  1. Make error messages clearly visible by...

    1. ... not just relying on color
      • Red text doesn't always work for color-blind users.
    2. ... using proximity
      • Show the message close to the related field.
    3. ... adding an indicator
      • E.g. an exclamation-marked triangle icon.

  2. Make it easy for the user to correct the problem

    1. Don't reset the form (blank-out fields) so users can fix typos instead of re-entering data.

  3. Use clear, friendly language when communicating

    1. Avoid accusatory language
      • “You did it wrong”
    2. Avoid vague or obvious messages
      • “Something went wrong”
    3. Explain why the data is wrong
      • “Email addresses should contain a '@'”

  4. Don't display technical errors (a.k.a. exceptions)

    1. “System.ArgumentNullException: Value cannot be null.”

We have the ability to make that moment the most frustrating interaction or the most pleasant by how we execute the validation and communicate the errors to the user. Do you agree? Have you thought about how error messages can affect a user's mood? Do you have any tips you'd like to share?
